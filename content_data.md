**Full Story (EN):**

The Great Wall of China is one of the most iconic structures in human history. Winding across mountains, deserts, and plains, it served as a military fortification, communication network, and transportation corridor for over two thousand years.

Construction began as early as the 7th century BC, when various Chinese states built walls to protect their own borders. It was Qin Shi Huang — the first emperor to unify China in 221 BC — who ordered these separate walls connected into a single defensive line. Hundreds of thousands of workers labored under brutal conditions. Many died and were buried within the wall itself.

Most of what survives today was built during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644 AD). The Ming emperors rebuilt and reinforced the wall using brick and stone, adding watchtowers, garrison stations, and smoke-signal systems. At its peak, over one million soldiers were stationed along its length.

Beyond its military function, the Great Wall became a profound cultural symbol — the boundary between the civilized Chinese world and the outside. Poets wrote about it. Painters depicted it. Today it is a symbol of unity, resilience, and extraordinary human will.

**Popular visiting sections near Beijing:**
- **Badaling** — Most visited, fully restored, accessible by subway
- **Mutianyu** — Scenic, less crowded, great for families
- **Jinshanling** — Semi-wild, perfect for photography and hiking
- **Simatai** — Open at night, UNESCO listed

**Full Story (ZH):**

中国长城是人类历史上最具标志性的建筑之一。蜿蜒穿越山脉、沙漠和平原，两千余年来作为军事防御工事、通讯网络和交通走廊发挥着重要作用。

长城的建造最早可追溯到公元前7世纪，当时各诸侯国建造城墙以保护自身边界。公元前221年，秦始皇统一六国后，下令将这些分散的城墙连接成一道统一的防线。数十万工人在艰苦条件下劳作，许多人死后就被埋葬在城墙之中。

今天保存下来的大部分长城建于明朝（1368–1644年）。明朝皇帝用砖石重建并加固了城墙，增加了烽火台、驻军站和烽烟通讯系统。鼎盛时期，超过一百万士兵驻守其沿线。

长城超越了军事功能，成为深刻的文化象征——中华文明世界与外部世界的界限。诗人为它写诗，画家为它作画。今天，它是团结、韧性和非凡人类意志的象征。

**Full Story (EN):**

At the heart of Beijing lies a city within a city. The Forbidden City was the imperial palace of China from 1420 to 1912 — home to 24 emperors of the Ming and Qing dynasties, and the seat of Chinese political power for nearly five centuries.

The numbers are staggering: 980 buildings, 8,728 rooms, 72 hectares of total area, and approximately 1.8 million artifacts in the collection. It is today the world's most visited museum, welcoming over 19 million visitors annually.

Nothing in the Forbidden City is random. The entire complex is oriented on a north-south axis aligned with the North Star — the heavenly counterpart of the emperor. Yellow roof tiles indicate imperial use (yellow was exclusively reserved for the emperor). Red walls represent luck and prosperity. Even the number of roof ornaments on each building communicates its occupant's rank.

The complex divides into two main sections: the Outer Court, where emperors received officials and conducted state ceremonies; and the Inner Court, the private living quarters of the imperial family, accessible only to royals and eunuchs.

**Visitor tips:** Book tickets in advance — daily capacity is capped at 80,000. Enter from Tiananmen Square in the south. Allow at least half a day; a full day for a thorough visit. The less-visited side halls contain extraordinary collections with almost no crowds.

**Full Story (ZH):**

在北京的心脏地带，有一座城中之城。故宫从1420年至1912年一直是中国的皇家宫殿，是明清两朝24位皇帝的居所，近五个世纪以来一直是中国政治权力的中枢。

其规模令人叹为观止：980座建筑、8728间房室、72公顷总面积、约180万件藏品。它是今天全球参观人数最多的博物馆，每年接待超过1900万游客。

故宫内没有任何随意之处。整个建筑群沿南北轴线布局，与北极星对齐——皇帝在天上的对应。黄色琉璃瓦代表皇家专属（黄色为皇帝专用）。红色宫墙象征好运与繁荣。每座建筑屋脊上的脊兽数量也传达着其主人的等级。

建筑群分为两个主要部分：外朝，皇帝在此接见官员、举行国家典礼；内廷，皇室家族的私人居所，仅皇族和太监可以进入。

**Full Story (EN):**

West Lake in Hangzhou is perhaps the most romanticized landscape in all of Chinese culture. Its image appears in classical poetry, ink paintings, silk embroidery, and porcelain from the Tang Dynasty to the present day.

The lake is divided by historic causeways into five main areas, each adorned with pagodas, pavilions, and garden islands. The famous Su Causeway — named after the Song Dynasty poet-official Su Shi, who ordered its construction — is best walked at dawn when mist rises from the water and fishermen cast nets in silence.

Ten classical viewpoints, known as the "Ten Scenes of West Lake," have been celebrated since the Southern Song Dynasty. Among the most famous: "Autumn Moon over the Calm Lake" (平湖秋月) and "Lotus in the Breeze at Broken Bridge" (断桥残雪).

A medieval explorer once called Hangzhou "the most beautiful and magnificent city in the world." Whether or not that crown still fits, West Lake remains a place where the boundary between reality and Chinese ink painting dissolves entirely.

**Full Story (ZH):**

杭州西湖也许是中国文化中最具诗意的风景。从唐朝至今，其意象出现在古典诗词、水墨画、丝绸刺绣和瓷器之中。

湖泊被历史悠久的堤坝分成五个主要区域，各有宝塔、亭台和花园岛屿点缀其间。著名的苏堤——以宋朝诗人兼官员苏轼命名，由他下令修建——最适合在黎明时分漫步，此时薄雾从水面升起，渔夫在寂静中撒网。

自南宋以来，"西湖十景"一直备受赞颂，其中最著名的包括"平湖秋月"和"断桥残雪"。这里是现实与中国水墨画之间界限完全消融的地方。

| **Short Description** | Over 8,000 life-size clay warriors buried with China's first emperor in 210 BC. One of the greatest archaeological discoveries of the 20th century, discovered accidentally by farmers in 1974. |

**Full Story (EN):**

In 1974, farmers digging a well near Xi'an broke through into a chamber filled with life-size clay soldiers. They had stumbled upon one of the greatest archaeological discoveries in human history — the Terracotta Army of Qin Shi Huang, the emperor who first unified China in 221 BC.

Three main pits have been excavated, containing over 8,000 warriors — infantry, archers, cavalry, generals, and charioteers — each with a unique face, hairstyle, and rank insignia. No two figures are identical. They were originally painted in vivid colors that faded within minutes of exposure to air.

The army stands guard over the emperor's main tomb mound, which has never been opened. Ancient texts describe rivers of mercury inside, simulating the rivers of China. Modern testing has confirmed elevated mercury levels around the mound. The decision not to excavate is deliberate — archaeologists are waiting for preservation technology to advance before opening what may be the most extraordinary burial site in history.

**Full Story (ZH):**

1974年，西安附近的农民挖井时意外凿入一个充满真人大小陶俑的地下室。他们偶然发现了人类历史上最伟大的考古发现之一——公元前221年统一中国的秦始皇的兵马俑。

三个主要坑道已经发掘，出土了8000余件武士俑——步兵、弓箭手、骑兵、将领和御者——每件面容、发型和军衔标志各不相同，无一重复。它们原本涂有鲜艳色彩，出土后因接触空气而在数分钟内褪色。

兵马俑守卫着皇帝从未被开掘的主墓。古籍记载墓中有水银之河，模拟中国的河流。现代检测已证实周围汞含量偏高。不发掘的决定是刻意为之——考古学家正在等待保护技术进步，再开掘这处也许是历史上最非凡的墓葬遗址。

Jiuzhaigou Valley is a 720 square kilometer nature reserve in the mountains of northern Sichuan. The valley takes its name from the nine Tibetan villages that once dotted its slopes. Its defining feature is over 100 lakes in colors that the mind refuses to accept as natural — turquoise, cobalt, jade, emerald, and ultramarine, all in the same valley, sometimes visible from the same viewpoint.

**Full Story (EN):**

The Summer Palace is the grandest imperial garden in China. At 2.9 square kilometers — three quarters of which is the man-made Kunming Lake — it served as the summer retreat of the imperial court, a place to escape Beijing's heat while conducting affairs of state.

The complex was originally built in 1750 as a birthday gift for an empress dowager, destroyed twice by foreign forces in 1860 and 1900, and rebuilt both times. Its most famous feature is the Long Corridor — a 728-meter covered walkway decorated with 14,000 paintings depicting scenes from Chinese history, mythology, and nature. Walking its length in silence, surrounded by paintings in every direction, is one of Beijing's most meditative experiences.

The Marble Boat at the western end of Kunming Lake is both beautiful and historically significant: it was built using funds diverted from the modernization of China's navy — a decision that contributed to a major military defeat in the late 19th century. The boat that never sailed became a symbol of imperial excess and national tragedy.

**Full Story (ZH):**

颐和园是中国规模最大的皇家园林。面积2.9平方公里——四分之三为人工开凿的昆明湖——是皇室的夏季避暑胜地，在暑热中处理国家事务的场所。

建筑群最初建于1750年，作为送给太后的生日礼物，先后于1860年和1900年被外国军队摧毁，两度重建。最著名的景点是长廊——728米长的有顶走廊，装饰着14000幅描绘中国历史、神话和自然场景的彩画。在寂静中走完全程，四周皆是彩画，是北京最令人沉思的体验之一。

昆明湖西端的石舫既美丽又具有重要历史意义：它用挪用自中国海军现代化建设的经费建造——这一决定促成了19世纪末的重大军事失败。这艘从未航行的船成为皇家奢靡和民族悲剧的象征。

When people imagine China, the Great Wall is often the first image that comes to mind. Stretching over 21,000 kilometers across northern China, it is one of the most recognizable structures in human history. But the wall is far more than a tourist attraction — it is a living testament to thousands of years of Chinese history, politics, and human determination.

## The Origins: Not One Wall, But Many

Contrary to popular belief, the Great Wall was not built all at once by a single emperor. Its construction spanned over two millennia, beginning as early as the 7th century BC when various Chinese states built walls to protect their borders from one another and from nomadic groups to the north.

It was the first emperor to unify China in 221 BC who ordered the connection of these existing walls into a single defensive line. Hundreds of thousands of workers — soldiers, peasants, and prisoners — labored under brutal conditions. It is estimated that over a million people died during this phase of construction, and many were buried within the wall itself.

## The Ming Dynasty: The Wall We See Today

Most of what survives today was built during the Ming Dynasty (1368–1644 AD). After the Mongols had ruled China, the Ming emperors were determined to prevent another northern invasion. They rebuilt and reinforced the wall using brick and stone, adding watchtowers, garrison stations, and smoke-signal systems for military communication.

## More Than Military: A Cultural Symbol

Beyond its military function, the Great Wall became a profound cultural symbol. It represented the boundary between the civilized world of China and the outside — a concept deeply embedded in Chinese identity. Poets wrote about it, painters depicted it, and generations of Chinese people saw it as the ultimate expression of national strength.

Today, the wall carries a different meaning. It is a symbol of unity, resilience, and the extraordinary things humans can achieve when driven by a common purpose.

## What Is Calligraphy?

Chinese calligraphy (书法, shūfǎ) is the artistic practice of writing Chinese characters using a brush and ink. Unlike Western calligraphy which focuses primarily on decorative lettering, Chinese calligraphy is considered fine art — equivalent in status to painting, poetry, and music.

The practice dates back over 3,000 years. The earliest form, oracle bone script (甲骨文), was carved into animal bones and turtle shells during the Shang Dynasty for divination purposes.

## The Five Script Styles

Over millennia, Chinese writing evolved into five distinct calligraphic styles:

1. **Seal Script (篆书)** — The oldest. Rigid and formal. Still seen on official seals and name stamps today.
2. **Clerical Script (隶书)** — Developed during the Han Dynasty. More angular and horizontal.
3. **Regular Script (楷书)** — The standard script used in printing today. What children learn first.
4. **Running Script (行书)** — Semi-cursive. Strokes begin to connect. The most common in everyday handwriting.
5. **Cursive Script (草书)** — Highly abbreviated and flowing. The freest artistic expression. The hardest to read.

## The Four Treasures of the Study

Traditional calligraphy requires the "Four Treasures of the Study" (文房四宝):

- **Brush (笔)** — Made from animal hair. The most important tool.
- **Ink (墨)** — Traditionally an ink stick rubbed on a stone with water.
- **Paper (纸)** — Xuan paper from Anhui province. Absorbs ink beautifully.
- **Ink Stone (砚)** — Used to grind ink and hold the liquid.

## Why It Still Matters

In the age of smartphones, calligraphy is not dying. Chinese schools still teach it. Millions practice it as meditation. Collectors pay fortunes for works by great masters. Every Chinese New Year, families paste hand-written couplets on their doors.

Calligraphy is not nostalgia. It is a living thread connecting modern China to its ancient soul.

- **Difficulty:** Beginner

**Key Takeaways:**
- Mandarin Chinese has 4 tones plus a neutral tone — the pitch pattern of a syllable changes its meaning completely, so tones are not optional

- The best way to learn tones is through minimal pairs (same syllable, different tones) combined with audio practice from a native speaker — reading alone is not enough
